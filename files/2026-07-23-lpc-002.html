Edition 002 · July 23, 2026 · Liberal Party of Canada — the Prime Minister's Office

The Centre Rebuilt

Carney's PMO after the July shuffle — who moved, who rose, and what the structure says.

The top line

In ten days of July, the Prime Minister's Office changed three of its most senior political posts. Scott Gilmore — Carney's senior adviser on foreign, defence and security policy — became principal secretary on July 22 [Canadian Press]. The man he replaced, Tom Pitfield, went to the Senate in the Prime Minister's first batch of appointments. And Maia Johnson took a role that has never existed at the centre of Canadian government: chief operating officer of the PMO, while keeping the Canada–U.S. file [Globe and Mail]. Deputy chief of staff Braeden Caley left days earlier to seek the Liberal nomination in North Vancouver–Capilano [CTV].

Each move is a story. Together they are a blueprint: a Prime Minister importing corporate structure into the centre, promoting security-policy weight into the top political-aide chair, and converting a campaign-era operative into an office-holder. The Hill Times reads the shake-up as fuel for cabinet-shuffle speculation [Hill Times].

What the full edition covers

1The Gilmore elevation — a security and foreign-policy pedigree in the principal secretary's chair, and what that says about where this PMO thinks the next two years will be fought
2The COO innovation — the first chief operating officer in PMO history: institutional design as a statement about how a former central banker runs power
3The Pitfield pipeline — operative to Senator: the oldest reward in Westminster politics, executed in the government's first Senate batch
4The Caley bet — staffer to candidate in North Vancouver–Capilano, and the PMO bench that has to backfill him
5The watchlist — the cabinet-shuffle signal, the campaign-director structure at the party, and the vacancies the shuffle leaves open
